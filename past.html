Since the early 1800s, the process of taking and developing photographs was a long one. A person would have to load the camera with film, use the film, then take it to a dark room and develop it using a variety of different chemicals, for various amounts of time. For over 100 years this was the only process of obtaining a photograph. Come 1923, a man named Samuel Shlafrock had the idea to include a miniature portable dark room with a film camera. Although this process never took off due to the difficulty of carrying around loose chemicals with an already bulky camera, it got people thinking about cameras that could instantly develop film.

Just over 20 years later, in 1948, a man named Edwin Land was out taking pictures of his daughter. Amid the picture-taking, the three-year-old asked her father why she is unable to view the photographs as he takes them. It was at this moment that Edwin Land thought up the idea of an instant camera.

At the time, Land, an ex-Harvard physics student who owned his own company, Land-Wheelwright Laboratories, along side his old physics professor, had an extensive background in the development of synthetic polarizers. Prior to the development of the polaroid camera, “the company [produced] glasses, ski goggles, 3D glasses, and dark-adapter goggles for the Army and Navy” (“Polaroid” n.p.). By February of 1947, Land created a working prototype, which he displayed at the Optical Society of America meeting, immediately captivating the audience with the futuristic invention. A year later, the first camera was purchased for $89.95, or about $974.00 today. Land’s company, now called Polaroid and no longer focusing on research but on technology development, began mass producing their cameras worldwide. Now people could take high quality, black and white photographs, and immediately hold the photo in their hands moments later, all due to a genius method developed by Edwin Land. The method changed slightly less than 10 years later when Land developed a color polaroid and released it to the public.

The process through which a polaroid camera works is similar to that of a regular film camera, the key difference being that the polaroid camera is loaded with a two-in-one type of film—film that acts as both the film and the paper on which the photo will be printed. When the user presses the shutter release or capture button, the camera slides a negative film sheet in front of the camera lens, and the shutter opens, capturing the image on the sheet.

Inside the film sheet are three layers of silver chemicals: a red layer to capture red light, a green layer to capture green light, and a blue layer to capture blue light. Separated from the silver chemicals is another reaction-inducing chemical contained in the white boarder (it’s not just for writing captions on the photograph!). As the camera pushes the picture out, rollers squeeze the reagent chemicals into the center of the sheet, and the reaction begins, developing the photograph right before the user’s eyes.
